How can we extract the perfect beans and make an espresso

Harvesting the Cherries


Every great espresso begins in the coffee fields, where ripe coffee “cherries” are hand-picked at the peak of maturity. Pickers move methodically down rows of trees, selecting only the bright red cherries that guarantee sweetness and balance. This selective hand-harvest preserves the quality of the crop and ensures consistency from tree to cup.


Processing and Drying


Once harvested, the cherries must be processed within 24 hours. In the washed process, the outer pulp is removed by machine, leaving the beans coated in a thin mucilage. They’re then fermented in water tanks for 12–48 hours to break down any remaining sugary layer. Following fermentation, the beans are carefully washed and spread out on raised beds or patios to dry under the sun, often being raked or turned regularly to ensure uniform moisture removal. This can take up to two weeks, depending on weather.


Hulling and Sorting


After reaching the ideal moisture level (around 12%), the dried “parchment” beans are hulled—removing the dried husk and parchment layer—revealing the green coffee beans. Advanced optical sorters and density tables then separate out any defects or under-developed beans. Quality control at this stage is critical: uniform bean size and density lead to more even roasting.


Roasting


Green beans arrive at the roastery ready for their transformative heat treatment. Master roasters develop precise profiles—temperature and time curves—to coax out each bean’s unique aroma and flavor notes (from bright citrus to dark chocolate). During roasting, beans undergo the “first crack” (expanding and melting sugars) and, if taken further, the “second crack” (developing deeper caramelization and body). For espresso, many roasters choose a medium‐to‐dark roast that balances acidity, sweetness, and crema production.


Grinding


Freshly roasted beans are ground immediately before extraction to preserve volatile aromatics. Espresso demands an extremely fine, uniform grind—almost powdery—to maximize surface area under high‐pressure water. Small adjustments to grind size allow baristas to tweak flow rate and extraction time, dialing in the perfect balance of strength and sweetness.


Tamping and Extraction


The measured dose of ground coffee is distributed evenly into a metal portafilter basket and tamped down with consistent pressure to form a compact “puck.” The portafilter locks into the espresso machine’s group head. Pressurized hot water (around 90–95 °C, at 9 bar of pressure) is forced through the puck for about 25–30 seconds. During this time, water extracts oils, acids, and soluble solids, producing a concentrated, velvety shot topped with a golden layer of crema—the hallmark of a well‐pulled espresso.


Serving


Finally, the espresso is poured into a pre-warmed cup to maintain temperature. A properly brewed shot will exhibit a rich aroma, a harmonious blend of sweetness and complexity, mellow acidity, and a silky mouthfeel—ready to be enjoyed straight or used as the base for lattes, cappuccinos, and other coffee creations.
